02 · Landscape

The market has consolidated

Three centers of gravity

Cluster 01
Creator clubhouse

Circle, Skool, Mighty Networks, Heartbeat. All-in-one space (forum, courses, events, payments) sold to creators, coaches, and cohort educators.

Compete on: bundle breadth, mobile polish, AI features. ~$50–$360/mo SMB, four figures for white-label.

Cluster 02
Open lobby

Discord, Slack. Synchronous chat at scale. Discord pushes hard into creator monetization. Slack has retreated from public communities.

Trade-off: huge DAU, weak structure for paid memberships, courses, and durable async content.

Cluster 03
Publishing-first

Substack Chat, Patreon Chats, Kajabi Communities. Community bolted onto a primary product (newsletter, podcast, course).

Win when: the primary asset is the publication and community is a retention layer, not the product.

Plus the B2B benchmark: Discourse and Bettermode remain the strongest answer when the buyer is a company (dev rel, customer community, partner program) rather than a creator.

03 · Deep dives
01 Creator clubhouse · Circle

The feature benchmark
for breadth.

What it is

"All-in-one community platform for creators and brands." Polished web app with deep customization. The default choice for course creators, coaches, and brand communities outgrowing a Facebook Group.

Headline features
  • Spaces (forum, chat, events, courses) with deep customization
  • Native paywalls, bundles, recurring subs, trials, coupons
  • Circle AI: agents, summaries, Activity Score
  • Branded mobile app on Plus tier
Pricing shape · verify

Roughly four tiers: ~$50 Basic · ~$100 Professional · ~$360 Business · $700+ Plus / branded app.

Recurring complaints
  • Mobile lags web; native app feels like a wrapper
  • Live chat is functional but feels secondary
  • Pricing escalates fast for branded app or zero-fee
  • Search and notification routing get noisy past a few thousand members
For Kyozo

Circle is the breadth benchmark. Match ~80% of the surface; don't try for 100% on day one.

02 Creator clubhouse · Skool

The price anchor.
And the affiliate flywheel.

What it is

Single opinionated layout: feed, classroom, calendar, members, leaderboard. Almost no configuration. Owned and championed publicly by Sam Ovens; ridden a heavy creator-affiliate flywheel since 2023.

What's working
  • Gamification (points, levels, content unlock) is the most copied idea in the cluster
  • Skool Games — public revenue leaderboard — drives evangelism
  • Mobile app improved fastest of any creator platform
  • Search and discovery are surprisingly good for the price
Pricing shape

Famously simple: ~$99/mo per community plus a transaction fee on paid memberships. Few tiers, few add-ons.

Where it falls short
  • Almost zero customization — the Skool look is the Skool look
  • Limited automation, weak API
  • Course features are basic vs. Kajabi/Teachable
  • Single-community model is awkward for creators with multiple paid offers
For Kyozo

Skool sets the price expectation in the prosumer creator segment. Anything materially more expensive needs a clear "why."

03 Creator clubhouse · Mighty Networks

The white-label play.

What it is

Self-serve Mighty product plus Mighty Pro, a higher-touch white-label branded-app program. Pitches itself as "cultural software."

Headline features
  • Spaces, courses, events, members, paid memberships, Stripe payouts
  • Best-in-class native iOS/Android in the cluster
  • Mighty Pro ships fully branded apps to App Store / Play Store
  • People Magic AI for member matching and content suggestions
Pricing shape · verify

Self-serve ~$41–$179/mo. Mighty Pro is custom and well into four figures monthly.

Where it falls short
  • Self-serve product evolves slower than Skool or Circle
  • Web UI density makes it feel heavy
  • Mighty Pro pricing makes it a different sale (and a different competitor) from the SMB tier
For Kyozo

Mighty Pro proves there is real budget for branded community apps. Treat white-label as a tier, not an afterthought.

03 · Deep dives
04 Open lobby · Discord

Distribution and a wildcard.

What it is

Default real-time chat for the internet. Has been deliberately courting creators since 2022 via Server Subscriptions, Premium Memberships, and Server Shop.

Strengths
  • Best-in-class voice/video/text at scale
  • Unmatched bot, slash-command, and developer ecosystem
  • Server Subscriptions + Premium Memberships + Server Shop
  • AutoMod, Stage channels, granular roles
Pricing shape · verify

Free to host. Discord takes a platform cut on Server Subscriptions (commonly cited ~10% after fees). Nitro is per-user.

Structural weaknesses
  • Async / long-form posts are second-class; threads aren't a forum
  • Identity is anchored to Discord — no portable member list
  • Search and discovery weak past a few thousand members
  • Platform risk: policy and take-rate changes can ripple through the entire creator cluster
For Kyozo

Don't compete on sync chat. Match quality, win on async, ownership, and creator-controlled monetization. Bridge in, don't replace.

03 · Deep dives

B2B benchmark

Where pricing power lives.

Open · OSS option
Discourse

The default forum software for OSS, dev-rel, and SaaS support. Mature long-form posts, plugin ecosystem, excellent SEO, granular trust levels, Discourse AI.

Pricing: hosted ~$50–$300/mo SMB, $1,000+/mo enterprise. Self-host is free.

Where it loses: not a sync chat product; aesthetic feels "forum software" to creators; onboarding is heavier than Skool.

Benchmark for content durability and openness.

Customer community
Bettermode (formerly Tribe)

Customer community platform for SaaS companies. Embeddable widgets, deep Salesforce / HubSpot / Zendesk integrations, Bettermode AI.

Pricing: SMB $200–$600+/mo, enterprise quoted. Higher than the creator cluster.

Where it loses: not a creator product; less momentum than Discourse in the same lane; UI feels like SaaS admin.

Benchmark for integrations and admin tooling.

Why this matters for Kyozo. The B2B quadrants pay 5–10× the creator cluster but require longer sales cycles and integration depth. Worth treating as year-2 expansion, not year-1 wedge.

05 · White space

Five unmet needs — recurring across reviews

What none of them actually solve.

Cross-community identity

Creators run multiple SKUs (free, paid, alumni, premium, partners). Members hate fragmented logins. No incumbent ships a unified inbox across communities.

True portability

Every SaaS player locks members and content into a proprietary schema. The SaaS economic model fights this — there's no commercial incentive to make leaving easy.

Sync + async parity

Discord nails sync. Circle nails async. Heartbeat is closest to both. None feel native at both modes simultaneously.

Programmability

Webhook and API surfaces are thin compared to what Slack offered ten years ago. Discord and Discourse are the only real exceptions.

Moderation that scales without hiring

AI moderation in this category is mostly summary tools, not action tools. The creator who runs three communities still ends up doing the hard work themselves.

06 · Positioning

Three credible positions for Kyozo

From narrowest to broadest.

Position A · Surface
Discord for grown-ups, Circle without the lock-in.
Polished SaaS community app, priced with Circle/Skool. Protocol is invisible to users — shows up as "you can export everything" and "your members own their participation."
Why it works Buyers don't need to understand the protocol to feel the benefits. Avoids the Mastodon trap. Risk Reads as "incremental Circle." Needs a real second proof point.
Position B · Long-term
The community protocol with a flagship app on top.
Lean into the protocol. Open standard for community membership, content, identity. Hosted app is the reference client. Court bridges from Discord/Slack/Discourse early.
Why it works Differentiation is unmistakable, defensible long-term. Strong with developer audiences. Risk Creator/SMB buyers don't care. Dual narratives are historically hard to run.
Wedge
Position C · Wedge
Operating system for someone with multiple communities.
Optimize for creators/brands running 3–10 community SKUs. One identity per member, one inbox, one analytics view, paid memberships that move between SKUs.
Why it works Real, painful, under-served job. No incumbent can ship this without re-architecting. Risk Smaller TAM early. Has to graduate into single-community use later.

Recommended composite

A on the surface,

In one sentence

A polished SaaS community app for people running more than one community, built on an open protocol so the member relationship is theirs.

The protocol is quiet in the marketing. It shows up as portability claims, embeddable widgets, and developer surface. Once the wedge customers are anchored, the protocol narrative steps forward to widen the platform — bridges from Discord/Slack, federation, and a published standard.

07 · Wedges

Five concrete wedges, ranked

Where to hit first.

  1. 01
    Migrations from Slack and Facebook Groups.
    Build the best importer in the category. Both audiences are actively shopping — Discourse owns the Slack/dev-rel migration; Circle owns the FB Group migration. Both importers are still mediocre.
  2. 02
    Cross-community identity.
    Sign in once, get a unified inbox across every Kyozo community a member belongs to. No incumbent can ship this without re-architecting; the most defensible early product wedge.
  3. 03
    Embeddable community.
    Bettermode and Discourse have proven the demand for community embedded in the product/site. Make it one line of JavaScript and price on member-seats, not page-views.
  4. 04
    Programmability and webhooks at every tier.
    Slack-grade developer surface, exposed for free or near-free, attracts the integrators and technical creators who set the tone for the platform.
  5. 05
    Member-owned export.
    Make every member able to download their own posts, messages, and relationships. SaaS vendors won't do this because it hurts retention. Kyozo can use it as a trust signal.

07 · Synthesis

The core bet

Three things have to be true.

01
The pain is real.
Creators running 3+ community SKUs are willing to switch to a single platform that solves identity and inbox fragmentation.
02
Polish matches incumbents.
UX and feature breadth land within ~80% of Circle on day one. Below that bar, "open" doesn't matter.
03
Protocol earns the next chapter.
Once the wedge is anchored, federation and bridges turn Kyozo from a SaaS app into the default substrate.
Failure modes to monitor

1. Bundle threats — Kit, Beehiiv, Kajabi keep adding "good-enough" community.   2. Discord ships native paid courses or async forums.   3. The protocol story consumes engineering bandwidth that should ship product polish.

08 · Asks

Decisions to make this quarter

Five open questions.

  • ¶
    Who are the first 100 customers?

    Multi-SKU creators is a sharp wedge but small. Comfortable selling there for 12 months, or do we need a parallel beachhead (dev-rel, OSS, coaching cohorts)?

  • ¶
    What's our take-rate posture?

    Match Skool's ~2.9%? Undercut at 0% on higher tiers like Circle? The answer materially changes GTM and product priorities.

  • ¶
    Is white-label a year-1 motion?

    Mighty Pro proves the budget exists. But the sale is longer and the build is custom. Year 1, or year 2?

  • ¶
    How visible is the protocol in marketing?

    Position A says "invisible." Position B says "front and center." This is the single biggest narrative decision in the deck.

  • ¶
    What's our AI thesis?

    Match the table-stakes summaries quietly, or pick one capability — e.g., a member-facing agent that acts across communities — and build a moat around it.

09 · Appendix

Methodology & caveats

How this was built.

Sources
  • Vendor pricing pages and product docs (publicly available)
  • G2 and Capterra reviews — recurring complaints, not headlines
  • Creator forum threads and migration-story posts
  • Public earnings, fundraising announcements, and product changelogs
Scope
  • Community SaaS (Circle, Skool, Mighty, Heartbeat, Geneva, Bettermode, Discourse)
  • Synchronous chat (Discord, Slack)
  • Creator/membership platforms with strong community features (Patreon, Substack, Kajabi)
Caveats
  • Pricing changes frequently. Verify each headline figure against the vendor's pricing page before quoting externally.
  • Take rates have asterisks. Discord, Patreon, Substack, and Skool all add payment-processing fees on top of the platform cut.
  • "AI" claims are aging fast. Most incumbents shipped material AI features in the last 12 months; treat the matrix as a snapshot.
  • Out of scope. Open communication protocols (Matrix, ActivityPub, AT Protocol, Nostr, Farcaster). Available as a follow-up if useful.
Confidence

High on positioning, structural strengths/weaknesses, and clusters. Medium on specific pricing figures and recent feature launches. Lower on user-base scale numbers; only cited where credibly published.
