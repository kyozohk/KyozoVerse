01 · Vision & mission

01 · Vision & mission

Own your audience. Fill the room.

The bet is structural. Instagram owns your followers. Eventbrite owns your ticket buyers. Substack owns the email addresses but not the relationship behind them. Kyozo owns nothing — the creative does. That difference is the shape no competitor will copy, because none of them are built for it.

The mission · the relationship layer in the AI stack

Notion is the page library. Claude is the reasoning layer. Kyozo is the relationship layer. Plugged into whatever AI client the user prefers, structured the way assistants will need to plan a member's week. That positioning is what every architectural decision in this document protects.

Why now

The audience asset is finally addressable. Direct relationships scale through AI rather than collapse under it. Two timing forces converge:

Member-side AI. Personal assistants are already planning weeks ("what's on this weekend from people I follow?"). Kyozo Pros' structured event data is the perfect signal — Substack, Patreon, Mailchimp don't have this shape.

Creative-side AI. Agentic features lift the audience-management work that doesn't need a human, leaving the creative free to do the part that does. Symbiosis, not substitution.

What we are building

A six-block product — Audience, Tags, Schedule, Guest List + RSVP, Broadcast, Inbox — plus a public Feed. Tags is the keystone: every action below pulls on it.

The loop: Feed → Audience → Tags → Schedule → Guest List → Broadcast → Inbox → Feed again. Audience data gets smarter every cycle. That is the product.

The platform shape: API-first, multi-tenant, event-emitting, MCP-ready. The seven invariants in section 12 exist because Phase 2 must be extension, not rewrite.

The investor read

Kyozo is the only platform that gives creatives an audience asset they own, structured for the AI clients members will increasingly use to plan their lives. Direct relationships, not rented attention. The architectural choices on the pages that follow are what make this defensible.

05 · Data-security commitments

05 · Data-security commitments

The promises behind the product.

These are the security commitments Kyozo makes to every creative who pays for Pro, and to every member who opts into a community. The four pillars are the surface; this page is the foundation under all of them. Every architectural decision in this brief protects one of these promises, and every row in the audit chain on page 09 maps back to one of them.

Commitment 01

Multi-tenant isolation · enforced at the database

Every Firestore read and write is scoped by communityId in security rules. Cross-tenant access is server-rejected, not hidden by client filters. communityMembers document IDs are pinned to ${uid}_${communityId} so membership cannot be forged. Admin SDK calls verify membership before privileged writes.

Auditable. Rules versioned in firestore.rules · last audit April 2026.

Commitment 02

Member consent · explicit and revocable

No one becomes a Member without explicit opt-in. Google SSO consent screen carries the community context. Opt-out is one button in every email and every member-facing surface. Member data leaving a community is the member's right, not a request to support. See diagram on page 07.

Auditable. Consent timestamp + provider stored on every Member record.

Commitment 03

Pluggable auth · humans, machines, AI clients

Firebase Auth handles humans (Google, Apple-ready, email). The kyz_* API-key system (in API_V1, hashed at rest, scope-gated) handles machines and MCP clients. One verifyAuth() adapter on every route — adding a new auth path is a small change, not a rewrite. Privilege-escalation paths via workspaceMembers have been closed.

Auditable. Every authenticated request logs principal type, scopes, resource touched.

Commitment 04

Domain-events table · every action recorded

Every state change (member opt-in, broadcast sent, feed post created, RSVP changed, agent action taken) writes a row in the events collection. Cheap now; the foundation for webhooks, integrations, agent triggers, anomaly detection, and audit replay. Being built this month.

Auditable. Immutable, append-only, replayable. Available to compliance review.

Commitment 05

Email + messaging done right

Resend handles email outbound (Amazon SES backing) and inbound. 360 Dialog handles WhatsApp. SPF / DKIM / DMARC configured automatically per community subdomain via the GoDaddy API. Webhooks signed and verified. Per-community mail reputation isolated. messages_whatsapp collection is server-only-write via Admin SDK.

Auditable. Delivery telemetry available per Pro · ~99.5% delivery observed.

Commitment 06

Breach-ready operations

Secrets rotated quarterly per SECRET_ROTATION_RUNBOOK.md. Sentry alerting on failed-auth bursts (in flight). CSP, HSTS, X-Frame-Options live. Dependabot / Renovate auto-PRs on CVE updates. Annual external security audit committed (next: August 2026).

Auditable. Runbook in repo · last rotation logged · next audit scheduled.

06 · Data handling architecture

06 · Data handling architecture

How data flows · how it's gated · where it lives.

The diagram below traces a typical request end-to-end. Three things never change: (1) every privileged write goes through a server-side route gated by verifyAuth and the Firestore rules, (2) tenant scope is enforced at the database, not the client, and (3) every external channel — Resend, 360 Dialog, GoDaddy — talks to the platform only through signed webhooks or server-side API keys, never the browser.

Solid arrows = privileged path (auth required). Dashed = optional or in-flight (MCP, partner, Sinch). The public Feed reads only visibility: public posts and never crosses the auth gate.

The principle. Every privileged write goes through the gateway, every gateway call verifies auth and rate-limits, every database row carries its tenant. The events table (right-bottom) closes the loop: every meaningful action gets a row, so webhooks, agents, integrations, and audit replay all read from one source of truth.

07 · Consent flow

07 · Consent flow

How a member opts in · how they opt out · what we record.

Consent is the first contract Kyozo makes with a member. The flow below is the same whether discovery starts on the Feed, a QR code, a friend's invite, or a direct link from a Pro. Three things stay constant: consent is explicit, consent is per-community, and consent is revocable from every member-facing surface.

Six explicit steps to consent · four ways to revoke · one audit trail. No silent opt-in, no email scraping, no list rental — ever.

What this rules out. Kyozo cannot become a member of a community on someone's behalf. CSV uploads create Contact records, not Member records — a contact only becomes a member after explicit opt-in via this flow. That distinction is structural and is enforced in the Firestore rules, not just in UI copy.

Thread 03 · Structural code · what's in place before the team grows
Practice 01

One canonical schema doc

/docs/schema.md generated from Zod, kept in sync via CI check. New entities go through PR review on schema before code. Pillar 01.

Practice 02

Code organisation by pillar

The repo is being reorganised into /src/pillars/{apis,app,agentic,mcp}/. Hires land in their pillar's folder; reviewers know where to look. Pillars 01–04.

Practice 03

Review discipline

Every PR has one reviewer + one signing-off-with-the-product-eye (often Will on agentic, Mary on UX). Reviews land in 24 hours. Cross-pillar.

Practice 04

Documentation standards

Every new endpoint ships with an OpenAPI entry, an event-emission line, a test, and a paragraph in /docs/changelog.md. No exceptions. Pillar 01.

Practice 05

Sprint cadence as a practice

Friday 30-minute slot. Three numbers (build success, error rate, weekly active Pros). Shipped / next / blocked in 3 lines each. Documented in repo. Cross-pillar.

Practice 06

Hire-readiness checklist

Before hire 02 starts: schema.md live, events table live, repo reorganised by pillar, two onboarding tasks pre-scoped. Their first week is a product test of our docs.

The principle

The structure has to scale before the team does. Every practice above is in place — or being put in place this month — so hires 02 and 03 are productive on day one. We are hiring into a system, not into chaos that needs a fixer.

13 · Architectural decisions · in writing

Direct answers to the ten architectural questions.

The questions Will put to Ashok in the V1 build brief, with answers grounded in the codebase today. The seven-invariant status table at the bottom shows what is done, what is partial, and what is being built before V1 ships.

Schema & data model · the core six

1. Is the web app a client of a backend API?

Partial. Today the web app is both a client of 35 serverless route handlers and a direct Firebase client for most reads. The pure "API-first, web is one of N clients" shape lands when API_V1 (Pillar 01) merges to main. Targeting end of Month 1.

2. One canonical schema doc?

To write. Firestore is NoSQL, so today the schema lives implicitly in TypeScript types, Zod validators, and security rules. Plan: schema.md in the repo by next Monday, generated from Zod. Will gets read access then.

3. Multi-tenancy from line one?

Yes. Every Firestore read/write is scoped by communityId in security rules; communityMembers use a ${'$'}{uid}_${'$'}{communityId} composite key so membership can't be forged. Cross-tenant access is server-rejected. Three legacy collections to close · ~1 day.

4. Domain-events table?

No — biggest gap. Webhooks fire for specific vendor events, but there is no canonical events collection. Cost to add: ~3 days. One Firestore collection, one emitEvent() helper called from every mutating route. Foundation for the rest.

5. Tags as a first-class entity?

Not yet — needs migration. Tags are currently a community subcollection / string array on members — the JSON-array pattern we flagged we'd regret. Plan: top-level tags collection + polymorphic tagAttachments join. ~1 week. Blocks Pillar 03 agentic features — prioritising.

6. Auth as a pluggable layer?

Partial. Firebase Auth supports Google, Apple, email natively. What's missing for true pluggability is API tokens for MCP and partner integrations. The kyz_* API-key system in API_V1 closes this gap.

Operational

7. Email deliverability · provider?

Resend, in production (with Amazon SES backing outbound). SPF/DKIM/DMARC configured automatically per community subdomain via GoDaddy. Bounces handled via webhook. ~99.5% delivery in the last 30 days.

8. Webhook endpoint per Pro · day one?

Not yet. Inbound vendor webhooks exist; outbound (Pro → external system) is not exposed. Plan: piggyback on API_V1 — adding a webhooks resource is ~3 days. Events table from Q4 feeds it.

9. Will building agent prototypes against the schema?

Yes. Read access to the repo + synced schema.md from Monday. Willer.fm is single-tenant by design. Output (prompts + data shapes) ports back behind feature flags. One agent per sprint after V1.

10. Friday sprint cadence · 30 minutes weekly?

Confirmed. Standing slot Friday 10am UK. Three numbers (build success rate, error rate, weekly active Pros). Shipped / next / blocked in 3 lines each. Documented in /docs/sprint-log.md.

14 · In closing

Boring choices in the foundation, defensible ones where it matters.

The architecture behind Kyozo is intentionally unflashy in the parts that have to work — Next.js, React, Firebase, Vercel, Resend, 360 Dialog. The defensible decisions are higher up: a custom domain per community, a unified inbox across every channel (web and Flutter), an API-first platform, and an MCP surface that turns Kyozo into a building block other AI tools call. That layering is what separates a defensible product from a thin CRUD app.

Twelve months of shipping (page 03) prove the team can execute. The audit chain (page 10) shows every commitment maps to a named accountable. The CEO and CTO own every decision on the pages above jointly. The team plan scales structure before headcount. Kyozo is being built to be the relationship layer in the AI stack — and the architecture is shaped to make that defensible, not just promised.

Will Roach · CEO  ·  Ashok Jaiswal · CTO  ·  Kyozo · May 2026
