Executive Summary — what this report says, in ten lines.
For the non-technical reader first.

What we built. What's next.
Why this is defensible.

Where we are today

Kyozo is rolling out V2 to a beta cohort and early adopters. Six product blocks — Audience, Tags, Schedule, Guest List + RSVP, Broadcast, Inbox — plus a public Feed, sitting on a unified loop architecture. Built in-house from July 2025, shipped as V2 in February 2026, security-hardened in April 2026. The four forward pillars take the company to Series A: APIs · a Unified App (Pro + Member, one codebase) · an Agentic AI strip · an MCP server that makes Kyozo addressable from any AI client.

Where we are going · in plain English

  • From web to everywhere. One backend; mobile, partners, AI clients all call it.
  • From manual to agentic. AI handles the audience work that doesn't need a human. The Pro stays human; the operations become machine.
  • From product to node in the AI agent network. Members increasingly plan their week through AI assistants. Kyozo is the relationship-layer primitive those assistants call.
  • From extractive to non-extractive. The data belongs to the Pro. Kyozo owns the runtime — not the data. This is the moat extractive competitors cannot retrofit.
A category of one · with caveats

Kyozo sits in the overlap between CRM and the creative sector — a segment incumbent CRMs underserve because they were built for corporate sales teams, not individual creators. Large CRMs (Salesforce, HubSpot) are wiring up MCP, but their architecture and pricing serve enterprise workflows. The relationship layer for creative practitioners is a different shape. The caveat: this only holds if we ship before someone else fills the gap.

What this document is

This document, the loop diagram, and the platform + PWA rollout strategy are the technological IP between Will and Ashok. Will's product vision and Ashok's architecture are the same artefact read from two angles. Together they form the bible Ashok ships against, the reference Will sells from, and the working library both update jointly.

The one-line read for the board

The relationship layer is the next primitive in the AI stack. Kyozo is being built to define it for the underserved creative sector — developing scalable, defensible technology that can extend further afield and be white-labelled. Architecture intentionally unflashy in the foundations; defensible at the layers that matter — custom domain per community, unified inbox, API-first, MCP-native, agent-extensible, non-extractive.

01 — Data security and the trust posture.
01 · Section status DONE · diagrams · SOC 2 timeline · CTO sign-off filled

The data is the Pro's.
The runtime is the product.

Non-extractive by design. Six lines we do not cross. Six technical commitments the architecture defends.

The strategic posture

Our posture is non-extractive. The data is the Pro's; the product is Kyozo's. Pros own the snapshot; we own the runtime that animates it. That is the whole position in one line.

The commitments behind the posture

  • Kyozo is a custodian, not a user. We do not use Pros' contact data for our own purposes — not enriched, not aggregated, not trained on, not sold.
  • The data is theirs — all of it. Pros own contacts and the organisational metadata they create (sublists, tags, segments, engagement patterns).
  • The product is the runtime. What we own is the engine: sublist intelligence, the polymorphic-tag graph, the time-decay engagement model, the agentic personalisation, the broadcast cadence. Outside Kyozo, the snapshot is just a file.
  • Contacts consent to a specific Pro. When a contact joins via Kyozo, they consent to share with that named Pro — not with Kyozo for our use, not with other Pros.
  • Bulk export is gated by a cooldown. A defined cooldown applies before hard-exporting contacts off-platform. Carve-out: GDPR Article 20 always-on.
  • Departure is clean. A Pro who leaves takes contacts (after cooldown) and metadata exports cleanly. No hostage situations.
The six lines we do not cross · structural, not just policy

Designed so they cannot happen, not merely policy-prohibited. Wired into auth scopes, query gates, isolation tests, audit events.

  1. No cross-Pro aggregation, even anonymised.
  2. No training on contact-level data.
  3. No third-party enrichment of contact data.
  4. No auto-discovery across Pros.
  5. No engagement data in ad targeting, including our own retargeting.
  6. No contact-level metadata exposed over MCP.

01 — The six technical commitments.
01 · Technical foundations

Six commitments.
Each one auditable.

01

Multi-tenant isolation · enforced at the database

Every Firestore read and write scoped by communityId in security rules. Cross-tenant access server-rejected, not client-filtered. Cross-tenant attack scenarios tested on every release.

Auditable: rules versioned in git · last audit Apr 2026 · next quarterly review Jul 2026.
02

Member consent · explicit and revocable

No one becomes a Member without clicking "Join [Pro]'s community" via Google SSO. Opt-out is one button in every email and member-facing surface.

Auditable: consent timestamp + provider stored on every Member record · exportable on request.
03

Pluggable auth · humans, machines, AI clients

Firebase Auth for humans. kyz_* API-key system for machines and MCP clients — hashed at rest, scope-gated. One verifyAuth() adapter on every route. Adding a new auth path is a 200-line change.

Auditable: every authenticated request logs principal type, scopes, resource touched.
04

Domain-events table · every action recorded

Every state change writes a row in the events collection. The foundation for webhooks, integrations, agent triggers, anomaly detection, audit replay. Being built this month.

Auditable: immutable, append-only, replayable · available to compliance review.
05

Email infrastructure done right

Resend handles outbound and inbound. SPF / DKIM / DMARC configured automatically per community subdomain. Per-community mail reputation isolated.

Auditable: delivery telemetry per Pro · 99.5% delivery observed last 30 days.
06

Breach-ready operations

Secrets rotated quarterly per runbook. Sentry alerting on failed-auth bursts. CSP, HSTS, X-Frame-Options live. Dependabot / Renovate auto-PRs on CVE updates. Annual external audit committed (next: Aug 2026).

Auditable: runbook in repo · last rotation logged · next audit scheduled.

Strategic risk · custom domain per community · "the bad actor"

The exposure. Every paying Pro gets handle.kyozo.com with auto-provisioned SPF, DKIM, MX. If community-X.kyozo.com starts sending spam, malicious links, or content that triggers complaint-rate breaches at Gmail / Outlook / Yahoo, the question is whether that subdomain's reputation can leak into the parent kyozo.com reputation, and what we do before it does.

The narrative behind the scoreboard

Two sentences per invariant — where we are, why it matters, which forward pillar it unblocks.

  1. API-first, web is a client. Today the web app reads Firestore directly for some flows and goes through 35 serverless routes for others — fine for V1 but blocks the mobile, MCP, and partner clients that all need the same surface. Merging API_V1 (~3 weeks) unifies it: web becomes one of N clients of a single OpenAPI-documented surface, which is the precondition for Pillar 04 (MCP) and the durability of Pillar 02 (Unified App).
  2. Canonical schema, multi-tenant. Multi-tenancy is enforced at the database via composite-key ${uid}_${communityId} rules and cross-tenant tests run on every release — solid. What's missing is one canonical /docs/schema.md generated from Zod and CI-checked, which is the readiness gate for Hire 2 and the single source of truth for every future engineer (~1 day).
  3. Tags as first-class polymorphic. Tags live as a community subcollection today and as string arrays on member documents in legacy places — the JSON-array shape Will warned would block us. Lifting Tags to a top-level collection with polymorphic tagAttachments is the keystone for Pillar 03 (Tagger, Sublist agents) — ~1 week of disciplined migration, and the single most leveraged engineering item this quarter.
  4. Domain events table. Every meaningful state change should write an immutable row to an events collection — today only vendor webhooks fire and we have no audit replay. Building this (~3 days, in flight) is the foundation for Pillar 04 (event-driven MCP), the audit chain on page 10, and the trust posture in Section 01 — cheap now, expensive to retrofit.
  5. Pluggable auth. Firebase Auth handles humans today (Google, Apple-ready) and the kyz_* API-key system in API_V1 handles machines and MCP clients — one verifyAuth() adapter on every route. Once API_V1 merges, adding a new auth path is a 200-line change, not a rewrite — which is the gate for Pillar 04 (MCP) and the precondition for partner integrations.
  6. Email deliverability done right. Resend is live with per-community SPF/DKIM/DMARC automated via the GoDaddy API and Amazon SES backing for outbound, observing ~99.5% delivery — this invariant is done. The pillar it unblocks is Pillar 02 (Communicate · Inbox), and the remaining adjacent work is the per-community deliverability dashboard for Pros.
  7. Webhook endpoint per Pro. Inbound webhooks from Resend and 360 Dialog exist and are signature-verified; the outbound surface — letting a Pro subscribe to their own community's events — does not yet exist. Building this (~3 days, piggybacking on API_V1 and the events table from Inv 4) closes the loop for Pillar 01 and is the entry point for partner integrations.

Total work to V1-compliant: ~3 weeks of focused engineering. Tags migration (Inv 3) and the events table (Inv 4) are the only items without working code somewhere. The rest is merging API_V1, writing the schema doc, and closing the legacy multi-tenancy gaps. Phase 2 is extension, not rewrite.

Technical summary

Seven invariants from the V1 brief. Five are done or have working code somewhere. Two — Tags-as-entity migration and domain-events table — are the only structural items still to build. ~3 weeks of focused work to V1-compliant. The data-security spine (Issues 1, 3, 4, 6) maps directly to 01.

08 — Strategic narrative. Why this design is the future.

Two futures.
The architecture is calibrated for one.

Future A — the SaaS path Kyozo is not on. Future B — Kyozo as the relationship primitive in the AI era.

Future A · the SaaS path we are not on

In this future, Kyozo is a SaaS. Mailchimp ships RSVP. Eventbrite ships guest lists. Substack ships paid memberships. Each major incumbent picks up one of our blocks. Kyozo gets crowded out the quarter after.

Future B · the relationship-layer primitive in the AI era

Kyozo's loop is built around data the Pro owns, runtime Kyozo owns, AI access mediated through Kyozo's per-Pro MCP keys. Every creative AI tool needs the relationship layer; Kyozo is what they call. Mailchimp can ship RSVP and they still don't have a relationship runtime. Eventbrite can ship guest lists and they still don't have an audience graph. Our competitors with extractive data models cannot retrofit the trust posture.

The one line that matters

Notion is the page library. Claude is the reasoning layer. Kyozo is the relationship layer. Plugged into whatever AI client the Pro prefers, structured the way assistants need to know a member's context.

That positioning is what every architectural decision in this document supports. The data posture, the loop architecture, the four forward pillars, the resource envelope — each calibrated for Future B.

In closing

Boring choices in the foundation. Defensible ones where it matters.

The architecture behind Kyozo is intentionally unflashy in the parts that have to work — Next.js, React, Firebase, Vercel, Resend. The defensible decisions are higher up: a custom domain per community, a unified inbox across every channel, an API-first platform, an MCP surface that turns Kyozo into a building block other AI tools call, and a non-extractive data posture that competitors with extractive economics cannot retrofit.

The CEO and CTO own every decision on the pages above jointly. The audit chain is independently verifiable from the repo and the Friday log. The team plan scales structure before headcount. The four pillars are the lens through which every future quarter will be read.

Kyozo is being built to be the relationship layer in the AI stack — and the architecture is shaped to make that defensible, not just promised.
